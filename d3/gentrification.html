How can we measure gentrification?

Gentrification can be a somewhat nebulous concept, but for the sake of consistency, we use the following methodology, from governing.com, to provide a specific definition.

To be eligible for gentrification, a tract's median income in 2000 must be below the 40th percentile.
This tract has a starting income below the threshold, so it is eligible.
Over 10 years, the median income rises considerably, but income change can be lagged, so it is not used as an indicator of gentrification.
To be considered gentrified, a tract must show significant growth in median home value (adjusted for inflation). In this tract, the median value is relatively low, leaving room to grow.
This line shows the minimum home value growth required for gentrification, which must be in the top third of growth rates in the city.
This tract shows greater growth, so it meets this criterion.
Finally, growth in educational attainment (number of four-year degrees) is used as a predictor of future income growth.
As with income, educational attainment growth rates must be in the top third percentile in the area.
Since educational attainment also comes in above the necessary growth rate, the tract is considered gentrified.
