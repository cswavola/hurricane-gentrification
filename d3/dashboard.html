Hurricane Katrina: Economic Impact & Gentrification

Gentrification is the process of transformation of a poor and disinvested community in an affluent direction. The changing economic status and incoming population often lead to displacement of existing residents. Typically this is a result of investors seeking low-cost opportunities for development, but the process can be jumpstarted by a catastrophic event, such as hurricane Katrina, whose damage pre-emptively drives out population, and necessitates significant new construction.

For this application, we rely on an analytic description of gentrification, focused on economic factors. Gentrification is defined by low initial median income (green) and median home value (pink), and a higher than average rise in both home value and educational attainment.

Above is an example of a census tract within New Orleans that has gentrified.

Low median income and median home value are the main qualifiers for gentrification eligibility. Among city census tracts, any neighborhood with both of these values falling below the 40th percentile for the city as a whole is eligible to gentrify.

While median income may rise, it often lags behind other changes, and its ending value is ultimately not a reliable indicator.

Because the tract displayed has a starting (2000) median income and home value both below the 40th percentile, it is eligible to gentrify.

Finally, to be considered gentrified, both home value (pink) and educational attainment (orange)—measured in the number of residents with at least a four year college degree—must rise more than the norm. Educational increase is a leading indicator of later income rise.

The dashed reference lines represent the 67th percentile of growth in each parameter. Growth in the top third percentile, falling above these lines, indicates that a neighborhood has gentrified, according to our definition.

Because the growth in home value and educational attainment are both above the reference norm, we consider the tract gentrified.
